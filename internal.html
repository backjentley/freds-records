Sales Intelligence
Our 7 Strongest Arguments
1
The 90-Day Exit — No Lock-In
This is the single strongest deal feature. Jim doesn't have to commit to anything permanent. If Month 3 doesn't show meaningful progress, he walks away with zero penalty, zero contract, and every line of code we wrote belongs to Fred's. This is the closer. Use it first when he pushes back on risk.
Objection it defeats: "What if it doesn't work?"
2
We Only Eat When Fred's Eats
10% of online revenue only. No retainer. No monthly fee from Summit. Zero dollars change hands until an online sale happens. Summit's incentives are perfectly aligned with Fred's. If we don't perform, we don't get paid. There is no scenario where Fred's loses money to Summit while online isn't working.
Objection it defeats: "How do I know you won't take money and disappear?"
3
The Diaspora: Customers Who Cannot Buy From You Today
There are estimated tens of thousands of Newfoundlanders in Alberta, Ontario, and internationally who grew up with Fred's and cannot walk through the door. This isn't speculative demand — it's existing brand loyalty with no purchase channel. Every dollar of diaspora revenue is genuinely new money. This is not cannibalizing in-store sales.
Market data: 330,000+ Newfoundlanders live outside the province. Platform vinyl spend: $85–$140 avg order.
4
The Vinyl Market Is Growing. Right Now.
Vinyl revenue surpassed CDs for the first time since 1987 in 2022. It has continued to grow every year since. Gen Z (18-24) is now the fastest-growing vinyl buyer demographic — 80% own a record player. Fred's is entering an expanding market, not a declining one. The question is not whether online record sales will grow — they will — but whether Fred's will capture any of it.
CAGR: 9.8% (2024-2028). Gen Z vinyl buyers: +22% YoY.
5
Tony Never Ships a Box
The operational burden question will come from Jim. The answer is airtight: Tony and the floor staff do exactly what they do today. The only change is a structured photography step when premium used records arrive. As online volume grows, the revenue it generates pays for a dedicated part-time packer. Tony's daily rhythm is protected by design, not by hope.
Objection it defeats: "This is going to overload the staff."
6
Sam the Record Man Opened Across the Street. It Closed. Fred's Didn't.
Fred's survived every format war, every chain store, and every industry collapse because of institutional adaptability. Fred Sr. launched Duckworth Distribution, signed to EMI, launched Atlantica Records — he moved before anyone asked him to. Going digital in 2026 is the same instinct. This is what Fred would have done. Use this with both Jim and the siblings. It reframes digital expansion as continuity, not disruption.
Response to: "Why change what's working?"
7
Fred's Owns the Building. The Online Store Costs Almost Nothing in Fixed Overhead.
Most retail businesses that go online carry rent, payroll, and inventory risk. Fred's already owns the building (acquired 1982 — the anchor that let them outlast HMV). The online store adds a revenue layer on top of a zero-rent infrastructure. The marginal cost of selling a record online vs. in-store is almost entirely packaging + shipping. This is one of the most capital-efficient digital expansions possible.
SaaS burn: $688/mo. Break-even: 46 records sold online.
