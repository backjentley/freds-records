The Jim Script
Seven Minutes That Matter

Format In-Person
Duration ~7 Minutes
Ask One Decision
Leave-behind admin.html
How to use this: Read the italicized script text as your spoken words — adapt naturally, don't recite. Director notes are in grey. Objection handlers are ready if he pushes back. The only goal of this conversation is a single yes: "Let's try it for 90 days."

Murray presents. Jack and Brandon are in the room for credibility but let Murray lead. Siblings should be aligned before this meeting — that's the buffer.
1
Opening · ~60 seconds
Honour the Legacy First
Before anything else, establish he's not being sold to
Note: Do not open with numbers, a deck, or technology. Open with him. The first 60 seconds determine whether he's listening or defending.
"Jim, I want to start by saying — we're not here to change anything. What you built at 198 Duckworth is something I'm genuinely proud to be part of. Fifty-four years. That doesn't happen by accident. It happens because someone made the right calls, every single year, when nobody else would. That's you. pause — let it land The only question we want to talk about today is: how do we make sure Fred's is still here in fifty more years? Because we think there's a way to do that. And it doesn't cost the store anything. Not a single record off the shelf. Not a change to how Tony and Matthew run the floor. Nothing."
Read the room: If Jim nods or says "okay" — continue. If he crosses his arms, say: "I know you've heard pitches before. This one is different because we only get paid if it works. If it doesn't work, you walk away and it costs you nothing extra." Then continue Beat 2.
2
The Gap · ~75 seconds
The One Thing That Doesn't Make Sense
Name the opportunity without making him feel bad about it
"Here's the thing that keeps Murray up at night. Fred's does over a million dollars a year in that store. That's an incredible number. One of the best independent record stores in the country — by revenue. pause And yet, right now, if someone in Alberta — a Newfoundlander who grew up buying records from you — goes online to find Fred's, to buy something, to send something home as a gift — there's nothing there. They can't buy from you. They move on. That's not a technology problem. That's a revenue problem. Those sales are happening — just not at Fred's. Somebody else is getting that money."
$1M+ In-Store Annually
$0 Online Revenue
54 yrs of Brand Trust
Note: Don't say "website is embarrassing" or reference any institution that used it as an example. The framing is purely about revenue left on the table — not about anything being wrong.
3
The Solution · ~90 seconds
What We're Actually Proposing
Plain English. No tech. No jargon.
"We want to build Fred's an online store. A proper one — not a generic website, but one that looks and feels like Fred's. Your photos, your history, your identity. pause The way it works is simple: Tony and Matthew keep running the floor the same way they always have. When a record comes in that's worth selling online — a rare one, a collectible — someone takes two photos on their iPhone. That's the new task. Two photos. Our team handles everything else from Ontario. We write the listing, price it against the market, and manage the whole digital side. pause Every Friday, those records go live to collectors across Canada and the US at the same time. It becomes a weekly event for buyers. They show up for it. Collectors love that kind of thing — it's the same reason people line up for Record Store Day. We call it the Friday Drop. And it requires almost nothing new from your team."
⚑ If he asks: "So who ships the boxes?"
"Who's doing the packing and shipping? Tony doesn't have time for that."
"Great question — and a fair one. In the early days, when volume is low, we handle that with whoever's available. As the online side grows, the revenue it generates pays for a part-time person whose only job is packing and shipping. The digital side funds its own operations. Tony never touches a box."
⚑ If he asks: "What if a customer buys it in-store and it's already sold online?"
"What happens if you sell a record in the store that someone already bought online?"
"We've solved this specifically. High-value records going online are held in a dedicated spot behind the counter — not in the regular bins — for the first 48 hours. The moment it sells in-store, it's gone from the website instantly. The system is designed so that can't happen."
4
The Deal · ~75 seconds
The Numbers — and the Risk
This should feel like a no-brainer, not a negotiation
"Here's what we're asking for. There's a setup cost — to build the store, get everything connected properly. That number is in the document we're leaving with you today, and it's a fraction of what a normal web agency would charge. pause After that, we take ten percent of whatever we sell online. Nothing from the store. Nothing from your in-store revenue. Not a dollar. Only from the new money we generate online together. pause If we sell nothing, you pay us nothing. We lose our time. You lose nothing. You can walk away after 90 days — no penalty, no contract. You keep everything we build. Every line of code, every customer list. It's yours. We've structured it this way because we believe in what we're doing. We're not here to take a fee and disappear. We only eat if Fred's eats first."
⚑ If he pushes back on cost
"That's still a lot of money for something unproven."
"I understand. And if you want to see the math: if we sell 50 records a month online — which is conservative given how many people follow this store — the software costs pay for themselves in the first week. We're not asking you to gamble on speculation. We're asking you to try something for 90 days with a clearly defined exit."
⚑ If he asks: "Who are you, exactly?"
"Why should I trust you with something like this?"
"Fair. We're a digital agency based in Ontario. We built the website for Meadus Contracting — a four-generation family builder — using the same heritage-first approach we're bringing here. We've also run sell-out streetwear drops and taken a fitness studio from zero to $42,000 in revenue in six weeks. We know how to do this. And we'd rather stake our income on it than charge you a retainer to prove it."
5
The Close · ~60 seconds
The Only Question Left
One ask. One answer. Don't over-speak.
"Jim, you've kept this store alive through the death of CDs, through Napster, through streaming, through a pandemic. You've outlasted every chain that ever tried to compete with independents. pause The vinyl market is bigger right now than it's been since 1983. A billion dollars a year in the US alone. The people who grew up in this store — who moved away — they still love Fred's. They just can't buy from Fred's yet. That's the only thing we're here to fix. We'd love to try this for 90 days, see what the numbers say, and go from there. That's all we're asking."
STOP TALKING. After the ask, be quiet. Silence is fine. Let him think. The worst thing you can do is fill the silence with more selling.
⚑ If he says: "Let me think about it"
"I need some time to think this over."
"Of course. Completely fair. We're leaving you this document — it has the full breakdown, the numbers, and every detail. No pressure. Take your time. The only thing I'd say is that every week without online sales is a week that money's going somewhere else. But we're here whenever you're ready."
⚑ If he says: "No"
"I'm not interested in doing this."
"I fully respect that. It's your business and your call. If anything changes or you have questions after you've had time to sit with it, Murray knows where to find us. Genuinely — thank you for your time today, Jim."
The Single Ask

One Decision.
One Sentence.

"Let's try it for 90 days. If the numbers aren't there, we shake hands and part ways. No hard feelings."
