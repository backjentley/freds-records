The Siblings Script
Getting to a United Front

Date Monday, March 23
Format Call or In-Person
Duration ~20 Minutes
Goal Active Advocates
This meeting is different from Jim's. Fred Jr. and the sister are not skeptics — they're already on Murray's side. The goal isn't to convince them it's a good idea. The goal is to make them feel seen in the plan, give them language to use on March 30th, and make sure they walk into that room excited, not just compliant.

Murray leads. Jack and Brandon support. Show them admin.html but lead with the parts about their future — not the risk mitigation language that's written for Jim.
Freddie Brokenshire Jr.
12 Years In-Store
Government Job
What he cares about: His dad's legacy. The store's survival. Whether he has a real future there. He's already doing the work — he just doesn't have a path to being properly compensated for it.
The Sister
Co-Owner
Kids, Other Commitments
What she cares about: Not being burdened. Protecting what the family built. Whether this complicates Jim's position or family dynamics. She needs to know nobody is asking her to do more work.
1
Opening · ~2 minutes
Start With What This Is Really About
They already know the problem. Don't oversell the setup.
Note: Murray delivers this part. Jack and Brandon are in the room to answer technical questions, not to pitch. The siblings trust Murray — they don't need to trust us yet.
"I want to show you guys something. Jack and Brandon have been working on this for the past week, and it's further along than I expected. pause Before we get into it — you both know the conversation we've been having for years. How do we grow this thing? How do we actually pay the people who built it what they deserve? How does Fred's look in another twenty years? This is the answer. And it doesn't require anyone to quit their job, take on debt, or convince Jim of anything he's not already open to hearing."
2
The Upside · ~5 minutes
What This Actually Means for the Family
Lead with ownership value — not just revenue. They're co-owners.
"Here's what Jack put together. The conservative case — and they've been deliberate about keeping these numbers low — is that within the first year, Fred's is generating forty to fifty thousand dollars a month online, on top of what it already does in the store. pause That's not a guess. That's based on what other independent record stores have done on Shopify, what the vinyl market is doing globally right now, and what Fred's brand is already worth to the diaspora — all those Newfoundlanders in Alberta and Ontario who can't buy from us today. Every dollar of that online revenue belongs to the four owners. It's new money — it doesn't touch the store, doesn't affect Jim's operations, doesn't change anything about how Tony and Matthew run the floor."
$42K–48K Conservative Monthly Online — Month 12
Distributed across four co-owners. New money on top of existing in-store revenue.
90 days Trial Period — No Long-Term Commitment
If the numbers aren't there after 90 days, walk away. No penalty. No contract.
10% Summit's Cut — Only on Online Revenue
Nothing from in-store. Nothing if it doesn't sell. Fully aligned incentives.
$0 Change to In-Store Revenue
The store runs exactly as it does today. This adds a layer; it doesn't replace anything.
⚑ If the sister asks: "Is this going to add work for me?"
"I have kids and a full-time job. I can't take on more."
"Nothing here falls on you. The only people whose daily life changes at all are Tony and Matthew — and even then, it's one structured photography step when premium records come in. Jack and Brandon run every part of the digital operation from Ontario. Your role is exactly what it's always been: co-owner."
3
Fred Jr. · ~4 minutes
What This Means for You Specifically, Fred
This is the most important beat. Speak directly to him here.
Note: Fred Jr. has 12 years of institutional knowledge in that store. He's the one person who could step into an operations leadership role as the digital side grows. Make him feel that — without overpromising. This is his moment to see himself in the plan.
"Fred — I want to say something directly to you. You've been in that store for twelve years. You know every supplier, every shelf, every process. There's nobody on earth who understands Fred's the way you do. pause The way we see this going: the digital side grows, volume grows, and the natural person to run that operation is someone who already knows every part of the business. That's you. Not right away — the revenue has to justify it first, and we want to prove it pays for itself before we make any decisions. But that's the direction. This is how you build a career at Fred's — not just a job. And that's what Dad would have wanted."
Do not frame Fred Jr.'s future role as a definitive plan. Nobody has had that conversation with him formally yet. This is about painting the possibility — giving him something to believe in. Aspirational language only.
⚑ If Fred Jr. asks: "So what would I actually be doing?"
"What does this look like for me practically?"
"Right now, nothing changes. You keep doing what you do. But as online volume grows — as we're shipping 50, 100, 200 records a month — someone needs to own that operation from the inside. That person needs to understand the store, the stock, the staff. We think that's you. When the revenue justifies the role, we want to build it around you."
4
The Legacy · ~3 minutes
What Your Dad Would Have Done
Emotional anchor. Only Murray can deliver this one.
Only Murray delivers this beat. Jack and Brandon should be quiet. This is a family moment. Do not interject with data.
"Dad built this place when he was nineteen years old. He figured out the music industry before anyone else in this province did. He took shots nobody else took — Duckworth Distribution, Atlantica Records, the East Coast Music Awards. He wasn't afraid to move. pause I think about that a lot. He would have been all over this. He would have had the e-commerce store running before anyone asked him to. That's the thing about Dad — he always saw the next thing coming. We have the same chance right now. The vinyl market is bigger than it's been since 1983. There are Newfoundlanders all over the country who love this store and can't buy from it. We can fix that. I want us to do this together — and then I want us to walk into that room with Jim and show him we're already aligned."
5
The Jim Meeting · ~5 minutes
How We Walk In on March 30th
This is what you're actually here to align on. The ask is their active participation.
"Here's what I need from you both on March 30th. Not a lot — but it matters. pause Jim is going to look at all three of us when we walk in. He needs to see that this isn't just my idea — that the whole family looked at this, asked the hard questions, and got comfortable with it. Your presence in that room is the signal. It tells him this is a family decision, not a pitch from two guys from Ontario. When he pushes back — and he will, on something — I need you both to be able to say: 'Jim, we looked at this. We asked those same questions. Here's what we found.' That's it. You don't have to sell him. You just have to be real."
What to Be Ready to Say on March 30th
  • "We've looked at this, and we think it's worth trying." The unified voice. This alone carries more weight than any data point.
  • "The 90-day trial means there's no lock-in. If it doesn't work, we walk away clean." Address his biggest fear directly — loss of control.
  • "Tony's job doesn't change. This adds something on top of what he already does." Jim trusts Tony. Anything that protects Tony's workflow protects Jim.
  • "The store doesn't change. We're just adding a way for people who can't get to Duckworth Street to still buy from Fred's." Frame it as extension, not transformation. Legacy language.
⚑ If the sister asks: "What if Jim says no?"
"If Jim doesn't go for it, what happens?"
"Then we respect it. Jim has majority say and we're not going around him. But the stronger our case is on the 30th, the more likely we get a yes — or at least a 90-day trial. That's all we're asking him for. One try."

"What if the online store looks bad and damages what Fred's has built?"
"It's a genuine concern — and it's why the design matters as much as the strategy. Jack, do you want to show them the mockup?" [Pull up Freds_MVP_Mockup.html or the main index.html here.]
